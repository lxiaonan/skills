# Frameworks

## A. Cash-Flow Map Workflow
Use when the user wants a monthly review or when any decision needs context.

1. **Collect monthly after-tax income**
   - Salary/wages
   - Business or side-hustle net income
   - Asset income: rent after expenses, dividends, interest, royalties, distributions
   - Irregular income: bonuses, gifts, commissions

2. **Classify outflows**
   - Survival essentials: housing, food, utilities, transport, medical, insurance, childcare
   - Debt payments: minimums, interest rates, extra payments
   - Lifestyle/discretionary: dining, shopping, subscriptions, travel, status spending
   - Growth spending: education, tools, business tests, certifications
   - Asset purchases: investments, business inventory, income-producing property

3. **List assets and liabilities**
   - Assets: liquid cash, retirement accounts, taxable investments, business equity, rental property net value, intellectual property, productive equipment.
   - Liabilities: credit cards, consumer loans, car loans, mortgage, student loans, tax obligations, business debt, BNPL/installments.

4. **Calculate simple indicators**
   - Monthly surplus = income - expenses - debt minimums.
   - Emergency runway = liquid reserves / essential monthly expenses.
   - Debt pressure = total minimum debt payments / after-tax income.
   - High-interest debt list = debts above a threshold that exceeds likely safe return.
   - Asset-income coverage = net asset income / monthly expenses.

5. **Diagnose pattern**
   - Negative surplus: repair cash flow before investing beyond essentials.
   - Positive surplus but no assets: redirect surplus into emergency fund, debt payoff, and learning.
   - Asset income present: reinvest intentionally and manage concentration risk.

## B. Asset vs Liability Decision Procedure
Use for “Is X an asset?” questions.

1. Identify purchase price and financing terms.
2. Estimate all recurring outflows: payment, interest, tax, insurance, maintenance, repairs, fees, subscriptions, time.
3. Estimate realistic inflows or savings: rental income, business revenue, resale value, lower costs, skill-based income uplift.
4. Separate **use value** from **cash-flow classification**.
5. Classify:
   - **Cash-flow asset**: expected net cash inflow after costs and risk allowance.
   - **Strategic capability asset**: may not produce immediate cash but credibly raises earning capacity; needs a learning or revenue plan.
   - **Lifestyle liability**: useful/enjoyable but produces net cash outflow.
   - **Speculative item**: depends mainly on resale appreciation; require caution.
6. State assumptions and what evidence would change the classification.

## C. Debt vs Investment Framework
Use for “Should I pay debt or invest?”

1. Gather debt APRs, balances, minimum payments, prepayment penalties, tax treatment if relevant, and emergency fund.
2. Segment debts:
   - High-interest/unstable debt: credit cards, payday loans, high APR consumer debt.
   - Medium-interest debt: personal loans, car loans, student loans depending on rate.
   - Low-interest/strategic debt: mortgage or subsidized debt, only if affordable.
3. Compare debt payoff return vs investment uncertainty:
   - Paying debt gives a risk-free return equal to interest avoided.
   - Investments have volatility, taxes, fees, and no guaranteed return.
4. Sequence default:
   - Pay minimums on all debts.
   - Build starter emergency fund.
   - Attack high-interest debt.
   - Continue basic retirement/employer match only if appropriate and stable.
   - After debt pressure is controlled, invest according to risk tolerance.
5. For borrowing to invest: classify as high risk; require professional advice and stress testing.

## D. Three-Path Action Plan
Use after mapping facts. Present all three, then recommend one.

### 1. 保守修复
For negative cash flow, no emergency fund, high-interest debt, unstable income, or high anxiety.
- Track every inflow/outflow for 30 days.
- Stop new consumer debt.
- Build starter emergency reserve.
- Renegotiate bills where possible.
- Pay down highest APR debt first or use snowball if motivation is the bottleneck.
- Learn one basic finance topic weekly.

### 2. 稳健优化
For stable surplus and manageable debt.
- Automate saving/investing after emergency reserve.
- Increase asset purchases gradually.
- Review insurance and risk protection.
- Build skill income: sales, negotiation, accounting, digital tools, industry expertise.
- Turn discretionary spending into intentional budget categories.
- Rebalance goals quarterly.

### 3. 进取学习
For strong runway, low debt pressure, and high learning capacity.
- Run capped side-hustle or investment experiments.
- Build a small business model canvas and test demand before scaling.
- Study market cycles, legal/tax basics, and risk management.
- Use position-size limits and stop-loss/exit rules where applicable.
- Consult licensed professionals before leverage, major contracts, or tax structures.

## E. 30/60/90-Day Coaching Plan

### First 30 days: visibility and stabilization
- Build a one-page cash-flow table.
- List debts with APRs and minimum payments.
- Identify top 3 cash leaks.
- Freeze or cap one lifestyle category.
- Set an emergency-fund target.
- Choose one financial-literacy topic: cash flow, debt, investing basics, or sales.

### Days 31–60: redirect surplus
- Automate a small transfer to emergency fund/debt payoff/investment depending on priority.
- Cancel or renegotiate low-value recurring expenses.
- Create an asset-building bucket.
- If side-hustle oriented: interview 5 potential customers before spending heavily.
- If investing oriented: write an investment policy statement before buying.

### Days 61–90: experiment and review
- Run one low-risk income or skill experiment.
- Review actual vs planned cash flow.
- Calculate emergency runway and asset-income coverage.
- Decide whether to stay conservative, optimize, or start a capped growth experiment.
- Write lessons learned and next 90-day goal.

## F. Side-Hustle Evaluation Loop
1. Problem: what customer problem is solved?
2. Customer: who pays, how often, and why now?
3. Cash needs: upfront cost, recurring cost, time cost, debt needed.
4. Unit economics: expected price, cost per sale, margin, payback period.
5. Skill gap: sales, operations, delivery, legal, accounting.
6. Downside cap: maximum money/time user can lose without harming essentials.
7. Experiment: smallest test that proves demand.
8. Scale rule: only increase spend after measured traction.

## G. Habit Review Loop
Use for repeated overspending or lifestyle inflation.
- Trigger: what emotion/event starts spending?
- Story: what belief justifies it? e.g., “I deserve it,” “everyone has one,” “it is an investment.”
- Cash-flow effect: immediate and recurring cost.
- Replacement: cheaper reward, delayed purchase, asset-first rule.
- Review: weekly spending reflection.
