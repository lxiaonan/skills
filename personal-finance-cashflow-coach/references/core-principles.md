# Core Principles

## 1. Cash flow is the primary lens
The core coaching question is not “Does this look valuable?” but “What does it do to monthly and long-term cash flow?” A decision can raise status while weakening cash flow. A purchase can be useful but still be a liability if it requires ongoing outflows without producing income.

Use this vocabulary:
- **Income statement view**: money in and money out each month.
- **Balance sheet view**: what the user owns and what the user owes.
- **Asset**: something that, after realistic costs, tends to put money into the user’s pocket or increases future earning capacity in a credible way.
- **Liability**: something that takes money out of the user’s pocket or creates mandatory future payments.
- **Asset income**: cash flow not directly tied to hourly labor, such as rental net income, royalties, dividends, business distributions, interest, or automated side-business profit. Risk and reliability vary.
- **Opportunity cost**: what the same money/time could have done if used to reduce debt, build reserves, buy income-producing assets, or learn a skill.

## 2. Rich-looking is not the same as financially stronger
A car, large home, luxury purchase, course, or business tool may feel like an asset. Classify it by net cash-flow effect:
- Does it create income or reduce necessary costs?
- Does it require debt, maintenance, insurance, taxes, subscriptions, repairs, or time?
- Can it be sold quickly without major loss?
- Does it improve earning power enough to justify the outflow?

## 3. Salary is useful, dependence is fragile
A stable job can fund learning, debt reduction, emergency reserves, and asset building. The blind spot is allowing every raise to become a larger lifestyle, mortgage, car payment, or consumer debt payment. The goal is not to shame employment; it is to redirect surplus from consumption into resilience and assets.

## 4. Financial education precedes risk expansion
Do not treat ignorance as courage. Before larger investments or entrepreneurship, the user should understand:
- Basic accounting: income, expenses, assets, liabilities, cash flow.
- Market logic: supply, demand, price, liquidity, cycles.
- Risk controls: emergency reserves, position size, diversification, downside limits.
- Legal/tax boundaries: jurisdiction-specific rules and professional advice.

## 5. Fear and desire distort money decisions
The source material emphasizes that fear of having no money and desire for status/comfort can create reactive decisions: taking any job, buying to impress, borrowing for lifestyle, chasing hot opportunities, or refusing to learn. Coaching should slow the user down: name the emotion, then analyze the cash flow.

Useful prompts:
- “If this purchase did not impress anyone, would you still buy it?”
- “Is fear pushing you into an action, or is a plan guiding it?”
- “What information would make this decision safer?”

## 6. Build the asset column before luxury expansion
A practical rule: recurring luxuries and big upgrades should ideally be funded by stable surplus or asset cash flow, not by high-interest debt or depletion of emergency reserves. This does not mean never enjoying life; it means sequencing consumption after resilience.

## 7. Small experiments beat blind leaps
For investing or entrepreneurship, favor experiments that teach while limiting downside:
- Test a side hustle with a capped budget and measurable demand.
- Learn sales/accounting/marketing before committing large capital.
- Paper-track an investment thesis before using meaningful money.
- Start with low-stakes education and only scale after evidence.

## 8. Wealth metric: survival time and freedom ratio
A useful metric is how long the user could live if work income stopped.
- **Survival months** = liquid emergency assets / monthly essential expenses.
- **Asset-income coverage ratio** = monthly net asset income / monthly expenses.
Financial freedom improves as survival months and asset-income coverage rise.

## 9. Anti-patterns to watch for
- Treating a personal home, car, or luxury as an asset without counting outflows.
- Increasing spending every time income rises.
- Borrowing to invest without understanding downside.
- Chasing “hot” tips, guaranteed returns, or social-media gurus.
- Confusing net worth estimates with spendable cash flow.
- Avoiding money conversations because they feel uncomfortable.
- Waiting for perfect conditions instead of running small learning experiments.
- Outsourcing all judgment to advisors without basic literacy.

## 10. Coaching stance
Be practical, non-shaming, and data-driven. The user may have family duties, cultural expectations, health limits, or local constraints. The goal is to increase clarity and options, not to impose one lifestyle.
